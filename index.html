Motivation

At first I was tempted to start a philosophical debate about RPG rules in general and a justification of my attempt specifically. But this would soon turn out to take up more space than the entire rules to be presented. So this discussion must be provided elsewhere. Suffice it to say that my disinfatuation with current rules available stems from the fact that they are overly complicated for the little "fitness" they show for representing the genre or game world they supposedly apply to.

Two major issues that annoy in particular I want to address shortly in the next two section.

Scalability

One of the technical flaws of almost every single set of rules I have come across is the applicability of skills and difficulties as they progress. With this rule set, higher skill levels compare to higher difficulty level exactly as lower skills and lower difficulty level. At every level the rules yield the same probability.

One consequence of this, as it turns out, is that using rules for multiple actors or multiple targets happens (scales) smoothly. There are no odd jumps in probabilities when rules are applied to groups nor is there a need for a new type of rule. Thus separate rules that apply to massive or complex (large scale) situations are not necessary.

Dimensions

Many rule sets complicate situations, especially combat, because they address the complexity of tactical decisions by creating artificial dimensions. Strength, Combat skills and Hit points are the dominating techical terms. This unnecessary creation of dimensions should be avoided. Tactical decisions should only be applied to comprehensive dimensions, which directly relate to the reality modelled. This is done here in the hope that both simplicity and realism can be fostered at the same time.

Basic Rule

You roll a d5 until you roll a 1 and the count the number of dice that did not show up a 1. That is the same as the number of dice you actually rolled minus one. The higher you get, the better, therefore we call it the positive roll. This roll is dominated by the active character, or more generally, the actor. Of course, there is a negative roll that is associated with the passive resistance or impediment, which is handled in the same way. These two numbers are subtracted. The result will be a whole number (i.e. a positive or negative number without fractional part).
